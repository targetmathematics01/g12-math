Exercise 4.1

Problem 1

Let $\displaystyle \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix}$, $\displaystyle \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{c} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 7 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$. Find the following vectors.

(a) $\displaystyle 3\vec{a}$
(b) $\displaystyle 4\vec{b}$
(c) $\displaystyle \vec{a}-\vec{b}$
(d) $\displaystyle \vec{b}+\vec{c}$
(e) $\displaystyle 2\vec{b}+\vec{c}$
(f) $\displaystyle \vec{a}-2\vec{b}$
(g) $\displaystyle \vec{a}+\vec{b}-2\vec{c}$
(h) $\displaystyle 3\vec{a}-\vec{b}+\vec{c}$
Solution

(a) $\displaystyle 3\vec{a}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 3\vec{a} &= 3\begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 15 \\ -6 \\ -12 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(b) $\displaystyle 4\vec{b}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 4\vec{b} &= 4\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ -24 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(c) $\displaystyle \vec{a}-\vec{b}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{a}-\vec{b} &= \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 - 3 \\ -2 - (-6) \\ -4 - 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \\ -5 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(d) $\displaystyle \vec{b}+\vec{c}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{b}+\vec{c} &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 7 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 + 0 \\ -6 + 7 \\ 1 - 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(e) $\displaystyle 2\vec{b}+\vec{c}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 2\vec{b}+\vec{c} &= 2\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 7 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -12 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 7 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -5 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(f) $\displaystyle \vec{a}-2\vec{b}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{a}-2\vec{b} &= \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} - 2\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -12 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 10 \\ -6 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(g) $\displaystyle \vec{a}+\vec{b}-2\vec{c}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{a}+\vec{b}-2\vec{c} &= \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} - 2\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 7 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ -8 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 14 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ -22 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(h) $\displaystyle 3\vec{a}-\vec{b}+\vec{c}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 3\vec{a}-\vec{b}+\vec{c} &= 3\begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 7 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 15 \\ -6 \\ -12 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 7 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 7 \\ -14 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

Problem 2

Given vectors $\displaystyle \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix}$, $\displaystyle \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{c} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ p \\ q \end{pmatrix}$.

(a) Find the values of $\displaystyle p$ and $\displaystyle q$ such that $\displaystyle \vec{c}$ is parallel to $\displaystyle \vec{a}$.

(b) Find the value of scalar $\displaystyle k$ such that $\displaystyle \vec{a}+k\vec{b}$ is parallel to vector $\displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 10 \\ 23 \end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

(a) Since $\displaystyle \vec{c}$ is parallel to $\displaystyle \vec{a}$, we have $\displaystyle \vec{c} = m\vec{a}$ for some scalar $\displaystyle m$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ p \\ q \end{pmatrix} &= m\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} m \\ 2m \\ 7m \end{pmatrix} \\ \end{aligned}$

Equating the components, we get:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} m &= -2 \\ p &= 2m = 2(-2) = -4 \\ q &= 7m = 7(-2) = -14 \end{aligned}$

Therefore, $\displaystyle p = -4$ and $\displaystyle q = -14$.

(b) Let $\displaystyle \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 10 \\ 23 \end{pmatrix}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{a} + k\vec{b} &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} + k\begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1-3k \\ 2+4k \\ 7+2k \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

Since $\displaystyle \vec{a}+k\vec{b}$ is parallel to $\displaystyle \vec{v}$, their components must be proportional.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 1-3k &= 0 \cdot m \implies 1-3k = 0 \implies k = \frac{1}{3} \end{aligned}$

Checking the other components with $\displaystyle k = \frac{1}{3}$:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 2 + 4\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) &= \frac{10}{3} \\ 7 + 2\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) &= \frac{23}{3} \end{aligned}$

Since $\displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 10/3 \\ 23/3 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 10 \\ 23 \end{pmatrix}$, they are parallel. Thus, $\displaystyle k = \frac{1}{3}$.

Problem 3

Points $\displaystyle A, B, C$ and $\displaystyle D$ have position vectors $\displaystyle \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$, $\displaystyle \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$, $\displaystyle \vec{c} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 \\ 8 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{d} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$ respectively. Point $\displaystyle E$ is the midpoint of $\displaystyle BC$.

(a) Find the position vector of $\displaystyle E$.

(b) Show that $\displaystyle ABED$ is a parallelogram.

Solution

(a) Since $\displaystyle E$ is the midpoint of $\displaystyle BC$, its position vector $\displaystyle \vec{e}$ is:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{e} &= \frac{1}{2}(\vec{b} + \vec{c}) \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \left[ \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 7 \\ 8 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix} \right] \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 8 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 4 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(b) For $\displaystyle ABED$ to be a parallelogram, we need to show that $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{DE}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \overrightarrow{AB} &= \vec{b} - \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \\ \overrightarrow{DE} &= \vec{e} - \vec{d} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 4 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

Since $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{DE}$, $\displaystyle ABED$ is a parallelogram.

Problem 4

Points $\displaystyle A, B$ and $\displaystyle C$ have position vectors $\displaystyle \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$, $\displaystyle \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{c} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$ respectively. Find the position vector of point $\displaystyle D$ such that $\displaystyle ABCD$ is a parallelogram.

Solution

For $\displaystyle ABCD$ to be a parallelogram, $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{DC}$. Let the position vector of $\displaystyle D$ be $\displaystyle \vec{d}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{b} - \vec{a} &= \vec{c} - \vec{d} \\ \vec{d} &= \vec{c} - \vec{b} + \vec{a} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 - 5 + 2 \\ 1 - 1 - 1 \\ 4 - 2 + 4 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

The position vector of point $\displaystyle D$ is $\displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}$.

Problem 5

$\displaystyle K(1,-1,0)$, $\displaystyle L(4,-3,7)$ and $\displaystyle M(a,2,b)$ are collinear. Find $\displaystyle a$ and $\displaystyle b$.

Solution

Since $\displaystyle K, L,$ and $\displaystyle M$ are collinear, the vectors $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{KM}$ and $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{KL}$ are parallel. Therefore, $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{KM} = k\overrightarrow{KL}$ for some scalar $\displaystyle k$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \overrightarrow{KL} &= \overrightarrow{OL} - \overrightarrow{OK} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -3 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} \\ \overrightarrow{KM} &= \overrightarrow{OM} - \overrightarrow{OK} = \begin{pmatrix} a \\ 2 \\ b \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a-1 \\ 3 \\ b \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

By equating $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{KM} = k\overrightarrow{KL}$:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} a-1 \\ 3 \\ b \end{pmatrix} &= k\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

Equating the components:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 3 &= -2k \implies k = -\frac{3}{2} \\ a-1 &= 3k = 3\left(-\frac{3}{2}\right) = -\frac{9}{2} \implies a = -\frac{7}{2} \\ b &= 7k = 7\left(-\frac{3}{2}\right) = -\frac{21}{2} \end{aligned}$

Therefore, $\displaystyle a = -\frac{7}{2}$ and $\displaystyle b = -\frac{21}{2}$.

Exercise 4.2

Problem 1

For $\displaystyle \vec{p} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$, $\displaystyle \vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$, find:

(a) $\displaystyle \vec{q} \cdot \vec{p}$
(b) $\displaystyle \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}$
(c) $\displaystyle \vec{q} \cdot (\vec{p} + \vec{r})$
(d) $\displaystyle \hat{\text{i}} \cdot \vec{p}$
(e) $\displaystyle \vec{q} \cdot \hat{\text{j}}$
(f) $\displaystyle \hat{\text{i}} \cdot \hat{\text{i}}$
Solution

(a) $\displaystyle \vec{q} \cdot \vec{p}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{q} \cdot \vec{p} &= \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = (-1)(3) + (5)(2) = -3 + 10 = 7 \end{aligned}$

(b) $\displaystyle \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r} &= \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} = (-1)(-2) + (5)(4) = 2 + 20 = 22 \end{aligned}$

(c) $\displaystyle \vec{q} \cdot (\vec{p} + \vec{r})$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{q} \cdot (\vec{p} + \vec{r}) &= \vec{q} \cdot \vec{p} + \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r} = 7 + 22 = 29 \end{aligned}$

(d) $\displaystyle \hat{\text{i}} \cdot \vec{p}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \hat{\text{i}} \cdot \vec{p} &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = (1)(3) + (0)(2) = 3 \end{aligned}$

(e) $\displaystyle \vec{q} \cdot \hat{\text{j}}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{q} \cdot \hat{\text{j}} &= \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = (-1)(0) + (5)(1) = 5 \end{aligned}$

(f) $\displaystyle \hat{\text{i}} \cdot \hat{\text{i}}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \hat{\text{i}} \cdot \hat{\text{i}} &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = (1)(1) + (0)(0) = 1 \end{aligned}$

Problem 2

For $\displaystyle \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$, $\displaystyle \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{c} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$. Find:

(a) $\displaystyle \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}$
(b) $\displaystyle \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}$
(c) $\displaystyle |\vec{a}|^2$
(d) $\displaystyle \vec{a} \cdot \vec{a}$
(e) $\displaystyle \vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} + \vec{c})$
(f) $\displaystyle \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} + \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c}$
Solution

(a) $\displaystyle \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = (2)(-1) + (1)(1) + (3)(1) = -2 + 1 + 3 = 2 \end{aligned}$

(b) $\displaystyle \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a} &= \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 2 \end{aligned}$

(c) $\displaystyle |\vec{a}|^2$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} |\vec{a}|^2 &= 2^2 + 1^2 + 3^2 = 4 + 1 + 9 = 14 \end{aligned}$

(d) $\displaystyle \vec{a} \cdot \vec{a}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{a} \cdot \vec{a} &= |\vec{a}|^2 = 14 \end{aligned}$

(e) $\displaystyle \vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} + \vec{c})$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \left[ \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \right] \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= (2)(-1) + (1)(0) + (3)(2) = -2 + 0 + 6 = 4 \end{aligned}$

(f) $\displaystyle \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} + \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c} &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = (2)(0) + (1)(-1) + (3)(1) = -1 + 3 = 2 \\ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} + \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c} &= 2 + 2 = 4 \end{aligned}$

Problem 3

Find the angle between $\displaystyle \vec{m}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{n}$ if:

(a) $\displaystyle \vec{m} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$, and $\displaystyle \vec{n} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$

(b) $\displaystyle \vec{m} = 2\hat{\text{j}} - \hat{\text{k}}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{n} = \hat{\text{i}} + 2\hat{\text{k}}$

Solution

(a) Let $\displaystyle \theta$ be the angle between $\displaystyle \vec{m}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{n}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{m} \cdot \vec{n} &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = (2)(-1) + (-1)(3) + (-1)(2) = -2 - 3 - 2 = -7 \\ |\vec{m}| &= \sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 1 + 1} = \sqrt{6} \\ |\vec{n}| &= \sqrt{(-1)^2 + 3^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{1 + 9 + 4} = \sqrt{14} \\ \cos \theta &= \frac{\vec{m} \cdot \vec{n}}{|\vec{m}||\vec{n}|} = \frac{-7}{\sqrt{6}\sqrt{14}} = \frac{-7}{\sqrt{84}} = \frac{-7}{2\sqrt{21}} \\ \theta &= \cos^{-1} \left( \frac{-7}{2\sqrt{21}} \right) \end{aligned}$

(b) Let $\displaystyle \theta$ be the angle between $\displaystyle \vec{m}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{n}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{m} &= \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} , \quad \vec{n} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \\ \vec{m} \cdot \vec{n} &= (0)(1) + (2)(0) + (-1)(2) = -2 \\ |\vec{m}| &= \sqrt{0^2 + 2^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{5} \\ |\vec{n}| &= \sqrt{1^2 + 0^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{5} \\ \cos \theta &= \frac{\vec{m} \cdot \vec{n}}{|\vec{m}||\vec{n}|} = \frac{-2}{\sqrt{5}\sqrt{5}} = -\frac{2}{5} \\ \theta &= \cos^{-1} \left( -\frac{2}{5} \right) \end{aligned}$

Problem 4

Find $\displaystyle t$ if the given pair of vectors are (i) perpendicular (ii) parallel.

(a) $\displaystyle \vec{p} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ t \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$

(b) $\displaystyle \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ t+2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 2-3t \\ t \end{pmatrix}$

(c) $\displaystyle \vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix} t \\ t+2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{s} = \begin{pmatrix} t \\ -4 \end{pmatrix}$

Solution

(a) $\displaystyle \vec{p} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ t \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \text{(i)} \quad & \text{For perpendicular vectors, } \vec{p} \cdot \vec{q} = 0. \\ & (3)(-2) + (t)(1) = 0 \implies -6 + t = 0 \implies t = 6. \\ \text{(ii)} \quad & \text{For parallel vectors, their components are proportional.} \\ & \frac{3}{-2} = \frac{t}{1} \implies t = -\frac{3}{2}. \end{aligned}$

(b) $\displaystyle \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ t+2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 2-3t \\ t \end{pmatrix}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \text{(i)} \quad & \text{For perpendicular vectors, } \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0. \\ & (0)(2-3t) + (t+2)(t) = 0 \implies t^2 + 2t = 0 \implies t(t + 2) = 0. \\ & \therefore t = 0 \text{ or } t = -2. \\ \text{(ii)} \quad & \text{For parallel vectors, } \vec{a} = k\vec{b}. \\ & \implies (0)(t) - (t+2)(2-3t) = 0 \implies (t+2)(2-3t) = 0. \\ & \therefore t = -2 \text{ or } t = \frac{2}{3}. \end{aligned}$

(c) $\displaystyle \vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix} t \\ t+2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{s} = \begin{pmatrix} t \\ -4 \end{pmatrix}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \text{(i)} \quad & \text{For perpendicular vectors, } \vec{r} \cdot \vec{s} = 0. \\ & (t)(t) + (t+2)(-4) = 0 \implies t^2 - 4t - 8 = 0. \\ & \text{Using quadratic formula: } t = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 - 4(1)(-8)}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{48}}{2} = 2 \pm 2\sqrt{3}. \\ \text{(ii)} \quad & \text{For parallel vectors, their components are proportional.} \\ & (t)(-4) - (t+2)(t) = 0 \implies -4t - t^2 - 2t = 0 \implies -t^2 - 6t = 0. \\ & \implies t(t + 6) = 0. \\ & \therefore t = 0 \text{ or } t = -6. \end{aligned}$

Problem 5

Find $\displaystyle t$ if $\displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ t \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$ is perpendicular to $\displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 1-t \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

Since the vectors are perpendicular, their scalar product is zero.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ t \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1-t \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} &= 0 \\ 3(1-t) + t(-3) + (-2)(4) &= 0 \\ 3 - 3t - 3t - 8 &= 0 \\ -6t - 5 &= 0 \\ -6t &= 5 \\ t &= -\frac{5}{6} \end{aligned}$

Problem 6

$\displaystyle ABCD$ is a parallelogram with $\displaystyle AB$ parallel to $\displaystyle DC$. Let $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{AB} = \vec{a}$ and $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{AD} = \vec{b}$.

(a) Express $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{AC}$ and $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{BD}$ in terms of $\displaystyle \vec{a}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{b}$.

(b) Simplify $\displaystyle (\vec{a} + \vec{b}) \cdot (\vec{b} - \vec{a})$.

(c) Hence show that if $\displaystyle ABCD$ is a rhombus then its diagonals are perpendicular.

Solution

(a) Express $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{AC}$ and $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{BD}$:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \text{By triangle law, } \overrightarrow{AC} &= \overrightarrow{AB} + \overrightarrow{BC} \\ \text{Since } ABCD \text{ is a parallelogram, } \overrightarrow{BC} &= \overrightarrow{AD} = \vec{b}. \\ \therefore \overrightarrow{AC} &= \vec{a} + \vec{b}. \\ \text{For } \overrightarrow{BD}, \quad \overrightarrow{BD} &= \overrightarrow{AD} - \overrightarrow{AB} = \vec{b} - \vec{a}. \end{aligned}$

(b) Simplify $\displaystyle (\vec{a} + \vec{b}) \cdot (\vec{b} - \vec{a})$:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} (\vec{a} + \vec{b}) \cdot (\vec{b} - \vec{a}) &= \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} - \vec{a} \cdot \vec{a} + \vec{b} \cdot \vec{b} - \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a} \\ &= \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} - |\vec{a}|^2 + |\vec{b}|^2 - \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} \\ &= |\vec{b}|^2 - |\vec{a}|^2. \end{aligned}$

(c) If $\displaystyle ABCD$ is a rhombus, all its sides are equal in length, so $\displaystyle |\vec{a}| = |\vec{b}|$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \text{Therefore, } |\vec{b}|^2 - |\vec{a}|^2 &= 0. \\ \implies (\vec{a} + \vec{b}) \cdot (\vec{b} - \vec{a}) &= 0. \\ \implies \overrightarrow{AC} \cdot \overrightarrow{BD} &= 0. \end{aligned}$

Since the scalar product of the diagonals is zero, the diagonals $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{AC}$ and $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{BD}$ are perpendicular.

Exercise 4.3

Problem 1

Find a vector perpendicular to the following pair of vectors:

(a) $\displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$
(b) $\displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$
Solution

A vector perpendicular to two given vectors can be found by calculating their cross product (using the column vector method).

(a) Let $\displaystyle \vec{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{u} \times \vec{v} &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} (1)(3) - (1)(2) \\ (1)(1) - (3)(3) \\ (3)(2) - (1)(1) \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 - 2 \\ 1 - 9 \\ 6 - 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -8 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(b) Let $\displaystyle \vec{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{u} \times \vec{v} &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} (-1)(5) - (4)(1) \\ (4)(-1) - (3)(5) \\ (3)(1) - (-1)(-1) \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} -5 - 4 \\ -4 - 15 \\ 3 - 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} -9 \\ -19 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

Problem 2

Consider $\displaystyle \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$.

(a) Find $\displaystyle \vec{a} \times \vec{b}$.

(b) Find $\displaystyle \sin \theta$ using $\displaystyle |\vec{a} \times \vec{b}| = |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\sin \theta$.

Solution

(a) $\displaystyle \vec{a} \times \vec{b}$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} (-1)(-1) - (3)(0) \\ (3)(1) - (2)(-1) \\ (2)(0) - (-1)(1) \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 - 0 \\ 3 + 2 \\ 0 + 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(b) Find $\displaystyle \sin \theta$

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} |\vec{a} \times \vec{b}| &= \sqrt{1^2 + 5^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{1 + 25 + 1} = \sqrt{27} \\ |\vec{a}| &= \sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{4 + 1 + 9} = \sqrt{14} \\ |\vec{b}| &= \sqrt{1^2 + 0^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{1 + 0 + 1} = \sqrt{2} \\ |\vec{a} \times \vec{b}| &= |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\sin \theta \\ \sqrt{27} &= \sqrt{14}\sqrt{2} \sin \theta \\ \sin \theta &= \frac{\sqrt{27}}{\sqrt{28}} = \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2\sqrt{7}} = \frac{3\sqrt{21}}{14} \end{aligned}$

Problem 3

Prove that for any two vectors $\displaystyle \vec{a}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{b}$, $\displaystyle |\vec{a} \times \vec{b}|^2 + (\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b})^2 = |\vec{a}|^2|\vec{b}|^2$.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle \theta$ be the angle between vectors $\displaystyle \vec{a}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{b}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \text{We know that } |\vec{a} \times \vec{b}| &= |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\sin \theta \\ \text{and } \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} &= |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\cos \theta. \\ \text{Substitute these into the left hand side:} \\ |\vec{a} \times \vec{b}|^2 + (\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b})^2 &= (|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\sin \theta)^2 + (|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\cos \theta)^2 \\ &= |\vec{a}|^2|\vec{b}|^2\sin^2 \theta + |\vec{a}|^2|\vec{b}|^2\cos^2 \theta \\ &= |\vec{a}|^2|\vec{b}|^2(\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta) \\ \text{Since } \sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1, \\ &= |\vec{a}|^2|\vec{b}|^2(1) \\ &= |\vec{a}|^2|\vec{b}|^2 \end{aligned}$

Problem 4

Given points $\displaystyle A$, $\displaystyle B$ and $\displaystyle C$ with coordinates $\displaystyle (3, 5, 1)$, $\displaystyle (7, 7, 2)$ and $\displaystyle (-1,1,3)$.

(a) Calculate $\displaystyle \vec{p} = \overrightarrow{AB} \times \overrightarrow{AC}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{q} = \overrightarrow{BA} \times \overrightarrow{BC}$.

(b) What can you say about vectors $\displaystyle \vec{p}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{q}$?

Solution

(a) Calculate $\displaystyle \vec{p}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{q}$:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \overrightarrow{AB} &= \begin{pmatrix} 7-3 \\ 7-5 \\ 2-1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ \overrightarrow{AC} &= \begin{pmatrix} -1-3 \\ 1-5 \\ 3-1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ -4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \\ \vec{p} = \overrightarrow{AB} \times \overrightarrow{AC} &= \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ -4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} (2)(2) - (1)(-4) \\ (1)(-4) - (4)(2) \\ (4)(-4) - (2)(-4) \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 4 + 4 \\ -4 - 8 \\ -16 + 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ -12 \\ -8 \end{pmatrix} \\ \\ \overrightarrow{BA} &= -\overrightarrow{AB} = \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ -2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \\ \overrightarrow{BC} &= \begin{pmatrix} -1-7 \\ 1-7 \\ 3-2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -8 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ \vec{q} = \overrightarrow{BA} \times \overrightarrow{BC} &= \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ -2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} -8 \\ -6 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} (-2)(1) - (-1)(-6) \\ (-1)(-8) - (-4)(1) \\ (-4)(-6) - (-2)(-8) \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} -2 - 6 \\ 8 + 4 \\ 24 - 16 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -8 \\ 12 \\ 8 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(b) Comparing $\displaystyle \vec{p}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{q}$:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \text{We can see that } \vec{p} &= \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ -12 \\ -8 \end{pmatrix} \text{ and } \vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} -8 \\ 12 \\ 8 \end{pmatrix}. \\ \implies \vec{p} &= -\vec{q} \end{aligned}$

The vectors $\displaystyle \vec{p}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{q}$ have the same magnitude but are opposite in direction. They both represent the normal to the plane containing the triangle $\displaystyle ABC$, but their orientations follow the right-hand rule from different starting vertices.

Problem 5

The points $\displaystyle A(3,1,2)$, $\displaystyle B(-1,1,5)$ and $\displaystyle C(7,2,3)$ are vertices of a parallelogram $\displaystyle ABCD$.

(a) Find the coordinates of $\displaystyle D$.

(b) Calculate the area of the parallelogram.

Solution

(a) For a parallelogram $\displaystyle ABCD$, $\displaystyle \overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{DC}$. Let the position vector of $\displaystyle D$ be $\displaystyle \vec{d}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \overrightarrow{AB} &= \begin{pmatrix} -1-3 \\ 1-1 \\ 5-2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \\ \vec{c} - \vec{d} &= \overrightarrow{AB} \\ \vec{d} &= \vec{c} - \overrightarrow{AB} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 7 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 11 \\ 2 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

The coordinates of $\displaystyle D$ are $\displaystyle (11, 2, 0)$.

(b) The area of the parallelogram $\displaystyle ABCD$ is given by $\displaystyle |\overrightarrow{AB} \times \overrightarrow{AD}|$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \overrightarrow{AD} &= \vec{d} - \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 11 \\ 2 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ 1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} \\ \overrightarrow{AB} \times \overrightarrow{AD} &= \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ 1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} (0)(-2) - (3)(1) \\ (3)(8) - (-4)(-2) \\ (-4)(1) - (0)(8) \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 0 - 3 \\ 24 - 8 \\ -4 - 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 16 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} \\ \text{Area} &= |\overrightarrow{AB} \times \overrightarrow{AD}| = \sqrt{(-3)^2 + 16^2 + (-4)^2} \\ &= \sqrt{9 + 256 + 16} = \sqrt{281} \text{ units}^2 \end{aligned}$

The vector equation of a line is $\displaystyle \vec{r} = \vec{a} + t\vec{b}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \vec{r} &= \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} + t\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

Problem 2

(a) Find the Cartesian equation of the line with parametric equation $\displaystyle x = 3t + 1$, $\displaystyle y = 4 - 2t$, $\displaystyle z = 3t - 1.$

(b) Find the unit vector in the direction of the line.

Solution

(a) From the parametric equations, express $\displaystyle t$ in terms of $\displaystyle x, y, z$:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} t &= \frac{x-1}{3} \\ t &= \frac{y-4}{-2} \\ t &= \frac{z+1}{3} \end{aligned}$

Equating these gives the Cartesian equation:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \frac{x-1}{3} &= \frac{y-4}{-2} = \frac{z+1}{3} \end{aligned}$

(b) The direction vector of the line is $\displaystyle \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$. The magnitude is:

The unit vector in the direction of the line is:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} \hat{v} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{22}}\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{3}{\sqrt{22}} \\ -\frac{2}{\sqrt{22}} \\ \frac{3}{\sqrt{22}} \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

Problem 3

Find the equation of the plane:

(a) with normal vector $\displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$ and which passes through $\displaystyle (-1, 2, 4)$.

(b) perpendicular to the line joining points $\displaystyle A(2, 3, 1)$ and $\displaystyle B(5, 7, 2)$ and which passes through $\displaystyle A$.

(c) containing $\displaystyle A(3, 2, 1)$ and the line $\displaystyle x=1+t$, $\displaystyle y=2-t$, $\displaystyle z=3+2t$.

Solution

(a) Normal $\displaystyle \vec{n} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$ and point $\displaystyle \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$. Equation is $\displaystyle \vec{r} \cdot \vec{n} = \vec{a} \cdot \vec{n}$.

Passes through $\displaystyle A(2, 3, 1)$. Thus, $\displaystyle \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 3x + 4y + z &= 3(2) + 4(3) + 1(1) \\ 3x + 4y + z &= 6 + 12 + 1 \\ 3x + 4y + z &= 19 \end{aligned}$

The normal vector $\displaystyle \vec{n}$ is perpendicular to both $\displaystyle \vec{d_1}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{d_2}$:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} x + 3y + z &= 1(3) + 3(2) + 1(1) \\ x + 3y + z &= 3 + 6 + 1 \\ x + 3y + z &= 10 \end{aligned}$

The plane is parallel to vectors $\displaystyle \vec{d_1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\displaystyle \vec{d_2} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$. The normal vector $\displaystyle \vec{n}$ is:
