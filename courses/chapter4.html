Chapter 4: Vector Algebra

Target Mathematics

We have learned vectors in two-dimensional rectangular coordinate system. In this chapter, we will extend the system to three dimensions and we will use vectors to calculate angles, distances and areas. We will also apply vectors to geometrical problems of lines and planes.

4.1 Vectors in Three Dimensions

In the plane, each point is associated with an ordered pair of real numbers. In space, each point is associated with an ordered triple of real numbers. Through a fixed point, called the origin $O$, draw three mutually perpendicular lines: the x-axis, the y-axis and the z-axis.

A point $P$ in space is determined by an ordered triple $\ds (x,y,z)$ of real numbers as shown in the diagram. These numbers $\ds x, y, z$ are called the coordinates of $P$.

Example 1

Illustrate the points (a) $\ds A(0,3,0)$ (b) $\ds B(4,0,2)$ (c) $\ds C(-1,2,2)$.

Solution

Standard Unit Vectors in Three Dimensions

To represent vectors in space, we introduce the standard unit vectors $\ds \hat{\text{i}}$, $\ds \hat{\text{j}}$ and $\ds \hat{\text{k}}$ where

$\ds \hat{\text{i}} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{\text{j}} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{\text{k}} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$

$\ds \hat{\text{i}}$ represents the unit vector in the positive x direction, $\ds \hat{\text{j}}$ represents the unit vector in the positive y direction and $\ds \hat{\text{k}}$ represents the unit vector in the positive z direction.

Figure 4.2

Position Vectors in Three Dimensions

If $\ds \overrightarrow{OA}$ is a vector with initial point at the origin $O$ and terminal point at point $\ds A(2,3,4)$, then we can represent $\ds \overrightarrow{OA}$ in terms of the vectors $\ds \hat{\text{i}}$, $\ds \hat{\text{j}}$ and $\ds \hat{\text{k}}$ as $\ds \overrightarrow{OA} = 2\hat{\text{i}} + 3\hat{\text{j}} + 4\hat{\text{k}}$.

We can also represent vector in three dimensions using column vector as like in two dimensions: $\ds \overrightarrow{OA} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$. The numbers in each column are called the components of the vector.

A vector whose initial point at the origin is called a position vector. For example, the position of point $\ds P(a,b,c)$ in the diagram can be represented by its position vector, $\ds \overrightarrow{OP}$.

$\ds \vec{p} = \overrightarrow{OP} = \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{pmatrix} = a\hat{\text{i}} + b\hat{\text{j}} + c\hat{\text{k}}$

The magnitude of a vector $\ds \overrightarrow{OP} = \vec{p} = \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{pmatrix}$ is given by

$\ds |\vec{p}| = \sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}$

In general, for the position vectors of two points $\ds A(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ and $\ds B(x_2,y_2,z_2)$, we can find the vector $\ds \overrightarrow{AB}$ as follows:

$\ds \overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{OB} - \overrightarrow{OA} = \begin{pmatrix} x_2-x_1 \\ y_2-y_1 \\ z_2-z_1 \end{pmatrix}$

and $\ds \overrightarrow{AB}$ is called the position vector of B relative to A.

Example 2

If P is $\ds (-3,1,2)$ and Q is $\ds (1,-1,3)$, find:

(a) $\ds \overrightarrow{OP}$
(b) $\ds \overrightarrow{PQ}$
(c) $\ds |\overrightarrow{PQ}|$
(d) $\ds \overrightarrow{QP}$
(e) $\ds |\overrightarrow{QP}|$
Solution

(a)

$\ds \overrightarrow{OP} = \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}.$

(b)

$\ds \overrightarrow{PQ} = \overrightarrow{OQ} - \overrightarrow{OP} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1+3 \\ -1-1 \\ 3-2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}.$

(c)

$\ds |\overrightarrow{PQ}| = \sqrt{4^2 + (-2)^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{21}.$

(d)

$\ds \overrightarrow{QP} = \overrightarrow{OP} - \overrightarrow{OQ} = \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -3-1 \\ 1+1 \\ 2-3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}.$

(e)

$\ds |\overrightarrow{QP}| = \sqrt{(-4)^2 + 2^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{21}.$

Algebraic Operations with Vectors

Negative Vector

The vector $\ds -\vec{a}$ has the same length as the vector $\ds \vec{a}$ but the opposite direction. When is given in component form, the components of $\ds -\vec{a}$ are the same as those for $\ds \vec{a}$ but with their sign reversed.

Figure 4.3

So if $\ds \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 11 \\ -6 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$, then

$\ds -\vec{a} = -\begin{pmatrix} 11 \\ -6 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -11 \\ 6 \\ -5 \end{pmatrix}$

Zero Vector

In three dimensional space, the zero vector is denoted by

$\ds \vec{0} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$

The Rules for Algebra with Vectors

The rule for algebra with vectors extend from two dimensions to three dimensions:

If $\ds \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ y_1 \\ z_1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\ds \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} x_2 \\ y_2 \\ z_2 \end{pmatrix}$, then

  • $\ds \vec{a} + \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ y_1 \\ z_1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} x_2 \\ y_2 \\ z_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1+x_2 \\ y_1+y_2 \\ z_1+z_2 \end{pmatrix}$
  • $\ds \vec{a} - \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ y_1 \\ z_1 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} x_2 \\ y_2 \\ z_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1-x_2 \\ y_1-y_2 \\ z_1-z_2 \end{pmatrix}$
  • $\ds k\vec{a} = k\begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ y_1 \\ z_1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} k x_1 \\ k y_1 \\ k z_1 \end{pmatrix}$ for any scalar k.

Example 3

If $\ds \vec{p} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\ds \vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$, find:

(a) $\ds \vec{p} + \vec{q}$
(b) $\ds \vec{p} - \frac{1}{2}\vec{q}$
(c) $\ds \frac{3}{2}\vec{q} - \vec{p}$
Solution

(a)

$\ds \vec{p} + \vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1-2 \\ -1+0 \\ 4+2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ -1 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}.$

(b)

$\ds \vec{p} - \frac{1}{2}\vec{q} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1+1 \\ -1-0 \\ 4-1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}.$

(c)

$\ds \frac{3}{2}\vec{q} - \vec{p} = \frac{3}{2}\begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -3-1 \\ 0+1 \\ 3-4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}.$

Equal Vectors and Parallel Vectors

Two vectors are equal if they have the same magnitude and direction. So, if arrows are used to represent vectors, then equal vectors are parallel and equal in length. This means that equal vector arrows are translations of one another; but in space.

If $\ds \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ y_1 \\ z_1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\ds \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} x_2 \\ y_2 \\ z_2 \end{pmatrix}$, then

$\ds \vec{a} = \vec{b} \iff x_1 = x_2, y_1 = y_2, z_1 = z_2$

Two vectors $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ are parallel if and only if

$\ds \vec{a} = k\vec{b} \text{ for some scalar } k$

Example 4

Find u and v given that $\ds \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ -1 \\ u \end{pmatrix}$ is parallel to $\ds \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} v \\ 2 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$

Solution

Since $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ are parallel, we have $\ds \vec{a} = k\vec{b}$ for some scalar k.

$\ds \begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ -1 \\ u \end{pmatrix} &= k\begin{pmatrix} v \\ 2 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} \\ -1 &= kv \\ -1 &= 2k \implies k = -\frac{1}{2} \\ u &= -2k \implies u = -2\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right) = 1 \\ -1 &= \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)v \implies v = 2 \end{aligned}$

Example 5

ABCD is a parallelogram. A is $\ds (-1, 1, 1)$, B is $\ds (2, 0, -2)$ and D is $\ds (3, 1, 4)$. Find the coordinates of C.

Solution

Let C be $\ds (x, y, z)$. Since ABCD is a parallelogram, AB // DC, and they have the same length, so

$\ds \begin{aligned} \overrightarrow{DC} &= \overrightarrow{AB} \\ \overrightarrow{OC} - \overrightarrow{OD} &= \overrightarrow{OB} - \overrightarrow{OA} \\ \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ \begin{pmatrix} x-3 \\ y-1 \\ z-4 \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix} \\ x-3 &= 3 \implies x = 6 \\ y-1 &= -1 \implies y = 0 \\ z-4 &= -3 \implies z = 1 \end{aligned}$

Therefore C is $\ds (6, 0, 1)$.

Unit Vector

A vector with magnitude 1 is called a unit vector. For a nonzero vector $\ds \vec{a}$, the unit vector in the direction of $\ds \vec{a}$, denoted by $\ds \hat{a}$ is given by

$\ds \hat{a} = \frac{\vec{a}}{|\vec{a}|}$

Example 6

(a) Find the unit vector in the same direction as $\ds \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}.$

(b) Find a vector of magnitude 5 that is parallel to $\ds \vec{a}.$

Solution

(a)

$\ds \begin{aligned} |\vec{a}| &= \sqrt{2^2 + (-2)^2 + 1^2} = 3 \\ \hat{a} &= \frac{\vec{a}}{|\vec{a}|} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2/3 \\ -2/3 \\ 1/3 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

(b) Let $\ds \vec{b}$ be parallel to $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds |\vec{b}| = 5$.

$\ds \begin{aligned} \vec{b} &= \pm 5\hat{a} \\ \vec{b} &= 5\begin{pmatrix} 2/3 \\ -2/3 \\ 1/3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 10/3 \\ -10/3 \\ 5/3 \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{or} \quad \vec{b} = -5\begin{pmatrix} 2/3 \\ -2/3 \\ 1/3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -10/3 \\ 10/3 \\ -5/3 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}$

Collinear Points

Three or more points are said to be collinear if they lie on the same straight line. A, B and C are collinear if $\ds \overrightarrow{AB} = k\overrightarrow{BC}$ for some scalar k.

Figure 4.4

Example 7

Prove that $\ds A(8,2,2)$, $\ds C(20,5,5)$ and $\ds B(12,3,3)$ are collinear.

Solution
$\ds \begin{aligned} \overrightarrow{AB} &= \overrightarrow{OB} - \overrightarrow{OA} = \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 3 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ \overrightarrow{BC} &= \overrightarrow{OC} - \overrightarrow{OB} = \begin{pmatrix} 20 \\ 5 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 3 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = 2\begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = 2\overrightarrow{AB} \end{aligned}$

Therefore BC is parallel to AB.

Since B is common to both, it follows that A, B and C are collinear.

4.2 Angle between Two Vectors and Scalar Product

First, we consider the angle $\ds \theta$ between the two vectors $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ for two dimensions. By using the cosine rule on the triangle formed by the vectors, we obtain:

$\ds \cos \theta = \frac{x_1x_2 + y_1y_2}{|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|}$

The expression $\ds x_1x_2 + y_1y_2$ can be written by $\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}$. That is,

$\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = x_1x_2 + y_1y_2$

for two dimensions.

This method can be extended into three dimensions. If $\ds \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ y_1 \\ z_1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\ds \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} x_2 \\ y_2 \\ z_2 \end{pmatrix}$, it can be written by

$\ds \cos \theta = \frac{x_1x_2 + y_1y_2 + z_1z_2}{|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|}$

Thus $\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}$ is also written by

$\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = x_1x_2 + y_1y_2 + z_1z_2$

The expression $\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}$ is called the scalar product or dot product of the vectors $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$.

In both cases, we can write

$\ds \cos \theta = \frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|}$

This result can be written in the form,

$\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\cos \theta$

Example 8

Find the angle between the two vectors $\ds \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\ds \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -12 \end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

Let $\ds \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\ds \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -12 \end{pmatrix}$.

$\ds \begin{aligned} |\vec{a}| &= \sqrt{3^2+4^2} = 5 \\ |\vec{b}| &= \sqrt{5^2+(-12)^2} = 13 \\ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} &= (3)(5) + (4)(-12) = 15 - 48 = -33 \\ \cos \theta &= \frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|} = \frac{-33}{5 \times 13} = -\frac{33}{65} \\ \theta &\approx 120.5^\circ \end{aligned}$

Example 9

Given points $\ds P(1,0,-1)$, $\ds Q(2,4,1)$ and $\ds R(3,5,6)$, find $\ds \angle QPR$.

Solution

The angle between $\ds \overrightarrow{PQ}$ and $\ds \overrightarrow{PR}$ is given by $\ds \theta$ in $\ds \cos \theta = \frac{\overrightarrow{PQ} \cdot \overrightarrow{PR}}{|\overrightarrow{PQ}||\overrightarrow{PR}|}$

$\ds \begin{aligned} \overrightarrow{PQ} &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \\ |\overrightarrow{PQ}| &= \sqrt{1^2+4^2+2^2} = \sqrt{21} \\ \overrightarrow{PR} &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} \\ |\overrightarrow{PR}| &= \sqrt{2^2+5^2+7^2} = \sqrt{78} \\ \overrightarrow{PQ} \cdot \overrightarrow{PR} &= (1)(2)+(4)(5)+(2)(7) = 2+20+14 = 36 \\ \cos \theta &= \frac{36}{\sqrt{21}\sqrt{78}} \\ \theta &= \cos^{-1} \left( \frac{36}{\sqrt{21}\sqrt{78}} \right) \approx 27.2^\circ \end{aligned}$

Algebraic Properties of the Scalar Product

If $\ds \vec{a}$, $\ds \vec{b}$ and $\ds \vec{c}$ are vectors in space and k is a scalar, then

  1. $\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}.$
  2. $\ds (-\vec{a}) \cdot \vec{b} = \vec{a} \cdot (-\vec{b}) = -(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}).$
  3. $\ds \vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) = \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} + \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c}.$
  4. $\ds (k\vec{a}) \cdot \vec{b} = k(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}) = \vec{a} \cdot (k\vec{b}).$
  5. $\ds \vec{0} \cdot \vec{a} = 0$

Geometric Properties of the Scalar Product

Let $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ be nonzero vectors.

  1. If $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ are perpendicular, then $\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0$
  2. If $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ are parallel in same direction, then $\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|$, in particular $\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{a} = |\vec{a}|^2$.
  3. If $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ are parallel in opposite direction, then $\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = -|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|$.

Example 10

Given that $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ are perpendicular vectors such that $\ds |\vec{a}|=3$ and $\ds |\vec{b}|=1$, evaluate $\ds (\vec{a}-\vec{b}) \cdot (\vec{a}+5\vec{b})$.

Solution

Since $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ are perpendicular, so $\ds \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a} = 0$.

$\ds \begin{aligned} (\vec{a}-\vec{b}) \cdot (\vec{a}+5\vec{b}) &= \vec{a} \cdot \vec{a} + 5(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}) - \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a} - 5(\vec{b} \cdot \vec{b}) \\ &= \vec{a} \cdot \vec{a} - 5(\vec{b} \cdot \vec{b}) \\ &= |\vec{a}|^2 - 5|\vec{b}|^2 \\ &= 3^2 - 5 \times 1^2 \\ &= 4 \end{aligned}$

4.3 Area of a Parallelogram and Vector Product

Vector Product (or) Cross Product

The vector product is only defined when both vectors are three-dimensional.

The vector product (or cross product) of $\ds \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ y_1 \\ z_1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\ds \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} x_2 \\ y_2 \\ z_2 \end{pmatrix}$ is given by

$\ds \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ y_1 \\ z_1 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} x_2 \\ y_2 \\ z_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} y_1z_2 - y_2z_1 \\ z_1x_2 - z_2x_1 \\ x_1y_2 - x_2y_1 \end{pmatrix}$

Instead of memorizing the formula for the vector product try using the following shortcut.

  1. Eliminate the component column that you are trying to calculate.
  2. Calculate: Down Product - Up Product.

$\ds \vec{a} \times \vec{b}$ is perpendicular to both $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$.

We can examine:

$\ds \begin{aligned} (\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{a} &= \begin{pmatrix} y_1z_2 - y_2z_1 \\ z_1x_2 - z_2x_1 \\ x_1y_2 - x_2y_1 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ y_1 \\ z_1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= (y_1z_2 - y_2z_1)x_1 + (z_1x_2 - z_2x_1)y_1 + (x_1y_2 - x_2y_1)z_1 \\ &= x_1y_1z_2 - x_1y_2z_1 + z_1x_2y_1 - z_2x_1y_1 + x_1y_2z_1 - x_2y_1z_1 = 0 \end{aligned}$

Therefore $\ds (\vec{a} \times \vec{b})$ is perpendicular to $\ds \vec{a}.$ Similarly, we can show that $\ds \vec{a} \times \vec{b}$ is also perpendicular to $\ds \vec{b}.$

Area of a parallelogram

Consider the parallelogram OACB lying on the plane. The area of OACB is given by

$\ds OA |\vec{b}| \sin \theta = |\vec{a}|(|\vec{b}|\sin \theta) = |\vec{a} \times \vec{b}|$

i.e., the area of the parallelogram with sides defined by vectors $\ds \overrightarrow{OA} = \vec{a}$ and $\ds \overrightarrow{OB} = \vec{b}$ is equal to the magnitude of the vector $\ds \vec{a} \times \vec{b}$.

Consequently, Area of triangle $\ds \alpha(\triangle ACB) = \frac{1}{2}(\alpha(OACB)) = \frac{1}{2}|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}|$.

Algebraic Properties of Vector Product

If $\ds \vec{a}$, $\ds \vec{b}$ and $\ds \vec{c}$ are vectors in space and k is a scalar, then

  1. $\ds \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = -(\vec{b} \times \vec{a}).$
  2. $\ds (k\vec{a}) \times \vec{b} = k(\vec{a} \times \vec{b})$
  3. $\ds \vec{a} \times (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) = (\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) + (\vec{a} \times \vec{c})$
  4. $\ds \vec{a} \times \vec{0} = \vec{0}$

Geometric Properties of the Vector Product

  1. If two nonzero vectors $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ are parallel, then $\ds \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \vec{0}.$ In particular, $\ds \vec{a} \times \vec{a} = \vec{0}$.
  2. If two nonzero vectors $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{b}$ are perpendicular, then $\ds |\vec{a} \times \vec{b}| = |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|$.

4.4 Lines and Planes in Space

Lines in Three-Dimensional Space

A direction vector of a straight line is a vector parallel to the line. In three dimensions geometry we can determined the equation of a line using its direction and any fixed point on the line.

Suppose a line passes through a fixed point A with $\ds \overrightarrow{OA} = \vec{a}$ and that the line is parallel to a vector $\ds \vec{b}$. Let R be any point on the line with $\ds \overrightarrow{OR} = \vec{r}$.

Then, $\ds \overrightarrow{OR} = \overrightarrow{OA} + \overrightarrow{AR}$. Since $\ds \overrightarrow{AR} // \vec{b}$, $\ds \overrightarrow{AR} = t\vec{b}$ for some $\ds t \in \mathbb{R}$.

So, vector equation of the line is:

$\ds \vec{r} = \vec{a} + t\vec{b}, \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$

Figure 4.6

In three dimensions, let $\ds \overrightarrow{OR} = \vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix}$, $\ds \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\ds \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{pmatrix}$

$\ds \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{pmatrix} + t\begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{pmatrix}$

is the vector equation of the line where $\ds R(x,y,z)$ is any point on the line, $\ds A(a_1,a_2,a_3)$ is the known (fixed) point on the line and $\ds \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{pmatrix}$ is the direction vector of the line.

Parametric Equation

Since we can write the parametric equation of the line

$\ds \begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} a_1 + tb_1 \\ a_2 + tb_2 \\ a_3 + tb_3 \end{pmatrix} \\ \begin{cases} x = a_1 + tb_1 \\ y = a_2 + tb_2 \\ z = a_3 + tb_3 \end{cases} &\text{ where } t \in \mathbb{R} \text{ is the parameter.} \end{aligned}$

Note: Each point on the line corresponds to exactly one value of t.

Cartesian Equation

By equating t values, we obtain the Cartesian equation of the line

$\ds \frac{x-a_1}{b_1} = \frac{y-a_2}{b_2} = \frac{z-a_3}{b_3}.$

Example 16

Find the Cartesian equation of the line with vector equation $\ds \vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} + t\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}.$

Solution

The vector equation is $\ds \vec{r} = \vec{a} + t\vec{b}$ therefore $\ds \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} + t\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$, $\ds t \in \mathbb{R}$

The parametric equations are:

$\ds \begin{aligned} x &= 1 + 3t \Rightarrow t = \frac{x-1}{3} \\ y &= 4 + 2t \Rightarrow t = \frac{y-4}{2} \\ z &= -1 + 5t \Rightarrow t = \frac{z+1}{5} \end{aligned}$

The Cartesian equation of the line is $\ds \frac{x-1}{3} = \frac{y-4}{2} = \frac{z+1}{5}.$

Example 17

Does the point $\ds A(3,-2,2)$ lie on the line with equation $\ds \frac{x+1}{2} = \frac{4-y}{3} = \frac{2z}{3}$ ?

Solution

Consider the given Cartesian equation passing through the point $\ds A(3,-2,2)$.

$\ds \begin{aligned} \frac{x+1}{2} &= \frac{3+1}{2} = 2 \\ \frac{4-y}{3} &= \frac{4-(-2)}{3} = 2 \\ \frac{2z}{3} &= \frac{2\times2}{3} = \frac{4}{3} \end{aligned}$

and so $\ds 2 = 2 \ne \frac{4}{3}$

The coordinates do not satisfy the Cartesian equation. Therefore, the point does not lie on the line.

ပြင်ညီများ (Planes in Three Dimensions)

To determine the vector equation of the plane, we requires extension of the ideas of the equation of line. If we fix two nonparallel vectors in that plane, the position vector of any point in the plane is a sum of scalar multiples of those two vectors.

Consider A, B and C be three noncollinear points on the plane with $\ds \overrightarrow{OA} = \vec{a}.$ Let $\ds \vec{d_1}$ and $\ds \vec{d_2}$ be the two nonparallel vectors on the plane. If the plane passing through A and having two nonparallel vectors $\ds \vec{d_1}$ and $\ds \vec{d_2}$, then the position vector $\ds \vec{r}$ of any point R on the plane is given by following equation.

Figure 4.7

Vector Equation of the Plane is

$\ds \vec{r} = \vec{a} + t_1\vec{d_1} + t_2\vec{d_2} \quad \text{where } t_1, t_2 \in \mathbb{R}. \quad (\overrightarrow{AR} = t_1\vec{d_1} + t_2\vec{d_2})$

Normal Vector

Any vector that is perpendicular to a plane is called a normal vector or simply normal to the plane. Normal is perpendicular to every line on the plane.

Figure 4.8

To find the different vector form of the equation of a plane, we can use the position vector of one point and the normal vector is perpendicular to the plane.

Thus, we consider a plane passing through a point A with position vector $\ds \vec{a}$ and $\ds \vec{n} = a\hat{\text{i}} + b\hat{\text{j}} + c\hat{\text{k}}$ is perpendicular to the given plane. Let $\ds \vec{r}$ be the position vector of an arbitrary point $\ds R(x,y,z)$ on the plane.

Since AR is a line in the plane, it follows that AR is at right angle to the normal vector $\ds \vec{n}$.

$\ds \begin{aligned} \vec{n} \cdot \overrightarrow{AR} &= 0 \\ \text{The vector } \overrightarrow{AR} \text{ is given by } \overrightarrow{AR} &= \vec{r} - \vec{a} \text{ and so} \\ (\vec{r} - \vec{a}) \cdot \vec{n} &= 0 \end{aligned}$

This is the vector equation of the plane. We can write this in an alternative form as

$\ds \begin{aligned} \vec{r} \cdot \vec{n} - \vec{a} \cdot \vec{n} &= 0 \\ \vec{r} \cdot \vec{n} &= \vec{a} \cdot \vec{n} \\ \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{pmatrix} &= \vec{a} \cdot \vec{n} \\ ax + by + cz &= d \quad \text{where } d = \vec{a} \cdot \vec{n}, \text{Note that d is a constant.} \end{aligned}$

The Cartesian equation of a plane has the form $\ds ax + by + cz = d$ where $\ds \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{pmatrix}$ the normal vector of the plane.

Example 18

Find the vector equation of the plane containing points $\ds M(2,2,-2)$, $\ds N(1,-1,3)$ and $\ds P(4,0,2)$.

Solution

Let $\ds \vec{a} = \overrightarrow{OM} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}.$ We can find that

$\ds \begin{aligned} \vec{d_1} &= \overrightarrow{MN} = \overrightarrow{ON} - \overrightarrow{OM} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ -3 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}. \\ \vec{d_2} &= \overrightarrow{MP} = \overrightarrow{OP} - \overrightarrow{OM} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}. \end{aligned}$

Therefore, the vector equation of the plane is

$\ds \vec{r} = \vec{a} + t_1\vec{d_1} + t_2\vec{d_2} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} + t_1\begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ -3 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} + t_2\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}.$

So, D does not lie on the same plane as A, B and C.
