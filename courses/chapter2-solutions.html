Problem 1(a)

Prove that $\displaystyle 1 + 3 + 3^2 + \cdots + 3^{n-1} = \frac{3^n - 1}{2}$ by using the principle of mathematical induction for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle 1 + 3 + 3^2 + \cdots + 3^{n-1} = \frac{3^n - 1}{2}$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle \text{LHS} = 1$ and $\displaystyle \text{RHS} = \frac{3^1 - 1}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1$.
Since $\displaystyle \text{LHS} = \text{RHS}$, $\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle 1 + 3 + 3^2 + \cdots + 3^{k-1} = \frac{3^k - 1}{2}.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle 1 + 3 + 3^2 + \cdots + 3^k = \frac{3^{k+1} - 1}{2}.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} & 1 + 3 + 3^2 + \cdots + 3^{k-1} + 3^k \\ & = \frac{3^k - 1}{2} + 3^k \\ & = \frac{3^k - 1 + 2 \cdot 3^k}{2} \\ & = \frac{3 \cdot 3^k - 1}{2} \\ & = \frac{3^{k+1} - 1}{2} \end{aligned}$

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 1(b)

Prove that $\displaystyle 1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + \cdots + n^3 = \left( \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \right)^2$ by using the principle of mathematical induction for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle 1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + \cdots + n^3 = \left( \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \right)^2$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle \text{LHS} = 1^3 = 1$ and $\displaystyle \text{RHS} = \left( \frac{1(1+1)}{2} \right)^2 = 1^2 = 1$.
Since $\displaystyle \text{LHS} = \text{RHS}$, $\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle 1^3 + 2^3 + \cdots + k^3 = \left( \frac{k(k+1)}{2} \right)^2.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle 1^3 + 2^3 + \cdots + (k+1)^3 = \left( \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2} \right)^2.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} & 1^3 + 2^3 + \cdots + k^3 + (k+1)^3 \\ & = \frac{k^2(k+1)^2}{4} + (k+1)^3 \\ & = \frac{(k+1)^2}{4} \left[ k^2 + 4(k+1) \right] \\ & = \frac{(k+1)^2(k^2 + 4k + 4)}{4} \\ & = \frac{(k+1)^2(k+2)^2}{4} \\ & = \left( \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2} \right)^2 \end{aligned}$

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 1(c)

Prove that $\displaystyle 2^3 + 4^3 + 6^3 + \cdots + (2n)^3 = 2n^2(n+1)^2$ by using the principle of mathematical induction for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle 2^3 + 4^3 + \cdots + (2n)^3 = 2n^2(n+1)^2$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle \text{LHS} = 2^3 = 8$ and $\displaystyle \text{RHS} = 2(1)^2(1+1)^2 = 2(4) = 8$.
Since $\displaystyle \text{LHS} = \text{RHS}$, $\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle 2^3 + 4^3 + \cdots + (2k)^3 = 2k^2(k+1)^2.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle 2^3 + 4^3 + \cdots + (2k+2)^3 = 2(k+1)^2(k+2)^2.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} & 2^3 + 4^3 + \cdots + (2k)^3 + (2k+2)^3 \\ & = 2k^2(k+1)^2 + (2(k+1))^3 \\ & = 2k^2(k+1)^2 + 8(k+1)^3 \\ & = 2(k+1)^2 \left[ k^2 + 4(k+1) \right] \\ & = 2(k+1)^2 (k^2 + 4k + 4) \\ & = 2(k+1)^2(k+2)^2 \end{aligned}$

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 1(d)

Prove that $\displaystyle 1 \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 3 + 3 \cdot 4 + \cdots + n(n+1) = \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3}$ by using the principle of mathematical induction for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle 1 \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 3 + \cdots + n(n+1) = \frac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3}$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle \text{LHS} = 1 \cdot 2 = 2$ and $\displaystyle \text{RHS} = \frac{1(1+1)(1+2)}{3} = \frac{6}{3} = 2$.
Since $\displaystyle \text{LHS} = \text{RHS}$, $\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle 1 \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 3 + \cdots + k(k+1) = \frac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{3}.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle 1 \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 3 + \cdots + (k+1)(k+2) = \frac{(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)}{3}.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} & 1 \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 3 + \cdots + k(k+1) + (k+1)(k+2) \\ & = \frac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{3} + (k+1)(k+2) \\ & = (k+1)(k+2) \left[ \frac{k}{3} + 1 \right] \\ & = \frac{(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)}{3} \end{aligned}$

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 1(e)

Prove that $\displaystyle \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{8} + \cdots + \frac{1}{2^n} = 1 - \frac{1}{2^n}$ by using the principle of mathematical induction for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \cdots + \frac{1}{2^n} = 1 - \frac{1}{2^n}$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle \text{LHS} = \frac{1}{2}$ and $\displaystyle \text{RHS} = 1 - \frac{1}{2^1} = \frac{1}{2}$.
Since $\displaystyle \text{LHS} = \text{RHS}$, $\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \cdots + \frac{1}{2^k} = 1 - \frac{1}{2^k}.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \cdots + \frac{1}{2^{k+1}} = 1 - \frac{1}{2^{k+1}}.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} & \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \cdots + \frac{1}{2^k} + \frac{1}{2^{k+1}} \\ & = \left( 1 - \frac{1}{2^k} \right) + \frac{1}{2^{k+1}} \\ & = 1 - \frac{2}{2^{k+1}} + \frac{1}{2^{k+1}} \\ & = 1 - \frac{1}{2^{k+1}} \end{aligned}$

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 1(f)

Prove that $\displaystyle 1 + 2 + 2^2 + \cdots + 2^{n-1} = 2^n - 1$ by using the principle of mathematical induction for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle 1 + 2 + 2^2 + \cdots + 2^{n-1} = 2^n - 1$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle \text{LHS} = 1$ and $\displaystyle \text{RHS} = 2^1 - 1 = 1$.
Since $\displaystyle \text{LHS} = \text{RHS}$, $\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle 1 + 2 + 2^2 + \cdots + 2^{k-1} = 2^k - 1.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle 1 + 2 + 2^2 + \cdots + 2^k = 2^{k+1} - 1.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} & 1 + 2 + 2^2 + \cdots + 2^{k-1} + 2^k \\ & = (2^k - 1) + 2^k \\ & = 2 \cdot 2^k - 1 \\ & = 2^{k+1} - 1 \end{aligned}$

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 1(g)

Prove that $\displaystyle \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3} + \frac{1}{3 \cdot 4} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n(n+1)} = \frac{n}{n+1}$ by using the principle of mathematical induction for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n(n+1)} = \frac{n}{n+1}$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle \text{LHS} = \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} = \frac{1}{2}$ and $\displaystyle \text{RHS} = \frac{1}{1+1} = \frac{1}{2}$.
Since $\displaystyle \text{LHS} = \text{RHS}$, $\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3} + \cdots + \frac{1}{k(k+1)} = \frac{k}{k+1}.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{(k+1)(k+2)} = \frac{k+1}{k+2}.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} & \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3} + \cdots + \frac{1}{k(k+1)} + \frac{1}{(k+1)(k+2)} \\ & = \frac{k}{k+1} + \frac{1}{(k+1)(k+2)} \\ & = \frac{k(k+2) + 1}{(k+1)(k+2)} \\ & = \frac{k^2 + 2k + 1}{(k+1)(k+2)} \\ & = \frac{(k+1)^2}{(k+1)(k+2)} \\ & = \frac{k+1}{k+2} \end{aligned}$

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 2

Prove that $\displaystyle (n + 1)^2 < 2n^2$ for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n \ge 3$ by using the principle of mathematical induction.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle (n + 1)^2 < 2n^2$ for $\displaystyle n \ge 3$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 3$,
$\displaystyle \text{LHS} = (3 + 1)^2 = 16$ and $\displaystyle \text{RHS} = 2(3)^2 = 18$.
Since $\displaystyle 16 < 18$, $\displaystyle P(3)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}, k \ge 3$, that is
$\displaystyle (k + 1)^2 < 2k^2.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle (k + 2)^2 < 2(k + 1)^2.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} (k + 2)^2 &= k^2 + 4k + 4 \\[4pt] &= (k^2 + 2k + 1) + 2k + 3 \\[4pt] &= (k + 1)^2 + 2k + 3 \end{aligned}$

By the induction hypothesis,

$\displaystyle (k + 1)^2 < 2k^2.$

Therefore,

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} (k + 2)^2 &= (k + 1)^2 + 2k + 3 \\[4pt] &< 2k^2 + 2k + 3. \end{aligned}$

Now, since $\displaystyle k \ge 3$, we have $\displaystyle 2k \ge 6$, so

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 2k + 3 &\le 4k + 2. \end{aligned}$

Hence,

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 2k^2 + 2k + 3 &\le 2k^2 + 4k + 2 \\[4pt] &= 2(k^2 + 2k + 1) \\[4pt] &= 2(k + 1)^2. \end{aligned}$

Thus,

$\displaystyle (k + 2)^2 < 2(k + 1)^2. $

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by the principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle (n + 1)^2 < 2n^2$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n \ge 3$.

Problem 3

Prove that $\displaystyle (2n + 7) < (n + 3)^2$ for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$ by using the mathematical induction.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle (2n + 7) < (n + 3)^2$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle \text{LHS} = 2(1) + 7 = 9$ and $\displaystyle \text{RHS} = (1 + 3)^2 = 16$.
Since $\displaystyle 9 < 16$, $\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle 2k + 7 < (k + 3)^2.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle 2(k + 1) + 7 < (k + 4)^2.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 2(k + 1) + 7 & = (2k + 7) + 2 \\ & < (k + 3)^2 + 2 \quad (\text{since } 2k + 7 < (k + 3)^2) \\ & = k^2 + 6k + 9 + 2 \\ & = k^2 + 6k + 11 \end{aligned}$

We know that $\displaystyle (k + 4)^2 = k^2 + 8k + 16$.

Since $\displaystyle k \ge 1$, we have $\displaystyle 6k + 11 < 8k + 16$.

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} k^2 + 6k + 11 & < k^2 + 8k + 16 \\ & = (k + 4)^2 \end{aligned}$

Thus, $\displaystyle 2(k + 1) + 7 < (k + 4)^2$.

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 4

Prove that $\displaystyle 3$ is a factor of $\displaystyle 4^n - 1$ for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$ by using the mathematical induction.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle 4^n - 1 \text{ is a multiple of } 3$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle 4^1 - 1 = 3$, which is a multiple of $\displaystyle 3$.
$\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle 4^k - 1 \text{ is a multiple of } 3.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle 4^{k + 1} - 1 \text{ is a multiple of } 3.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 4^{k + 1} - 1 & = 4 \cdot 4^k - 1 \\ & = 4(4^k - 1) + 4 - 1 \\ & = 4(4^k - 1) + 3 \end{aligned}$

Since $\displaystyle 4(4^k - 1)$ is a multiple of $\displaystyle 3$ (from the assumption) and $\displaystyle 3$ is a multiple of $\displaystyle 3$, their sum $\displaystyle 4(4^k - 1) + 3 = 4^{k + 1} - 1$ is also a multiple of $\displaystyle 3$.

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 5

Prove that $\displaystyle 3^{2n} - 1$ is divisible by $\displaystyle 8$ for all natural number $\displaystyle n$ by using the mathematical induction.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle 3^{2n} - 1 \text{ is divisible by } 8$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle 3^{2(1)} - 1 = 9 - 1 = 8$, which is divisible by $\displaystyle 8$.
$\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle 3^{2k} - 1 \text{ is divisible by } 8.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle 3^{2(k + 1)} - 1 \text{ is divisible by } 8.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} 3^{2(k + 1)} - 1 & = 3^{2k + 2} - 1 \\ & = 9 \cdot 3^{2k} - 1 \\ & = 9(3^{2k} - 1) + 9 - 1 \\ & = 9(3^{2k} - 1) + 8 \end{aligned}$

Since $\displaystyle 9(3^{2k} - 1)$ is divisible by $\displaystyle 8$ (from the assumption) and $\displaystyle 8$ is divisible by $\displaystyle 8$, their sum $\displaystyle 9(3^{2k} - 1) + 8 = 3^{2(k + 1)} - 1$ is also divisible by $\displaystyle 8$.

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 6

Prove that $\displaystyle n^3 - n + 3$ is divisible by $\displaystyle 3$ for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$ by using the mathematical induction.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle n^3 - n + 3 \text{ is divisible by } 3$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle 1^3 - 1 + 3 = 3$, which is divisible by $\displaystyle 3$.
$\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle k^3 - k + 3 \text{ is divisible by } 3.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle (k + 1)^3 - (k + 1) + 3 \text{ is divisible by } 3.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} (k + 1)^3 - (k + 1) + 3 & = k^3 + 3k^2 + 3k + 1 - k - 1 + 3 \\ & = (k^3 - k + 3) + 3k^2 + 3k \\ & = (k^3 - k + 3) + 3(k^2 + k) \end{aligned}$

Since $\displaystyle (k^3 - k + 3)$ is divisible by $\displaystyle 3$ (from the assumption) and $\displaystyle 3(k^2 + k)$ is divisible by $\displaystyle 3$, their sum is also divisible by $\displaystyle 3$.

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.

Problem 7

Prove that $\displaystyle x^{2n} - y^{2n}$ is divisible by $\displaystyle x + y$ for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$ by using the mathematical induction.

Solution

Let $\displaystyle P(n)$ denote the statement $\displaystyle x^{2n} - y^{2n} \text{ is divisible by } x + y$.

(1) For $\displaystyle n = 1$,
$\displaystyle x^{2(1)} - y^{2(1)} = x^2 - y^2 = (x + y)(x - y)$.
Since it contains the factor $\displaystyle (x + y)$, it is divisible by $\displaystyle x + y$.
$\displaystyle P(1)$ is true.

(2) Assume $\displaystyle P(k)$ is true for some $\displaystyle k \in \mathbb{N}$, that is
$\displaystyle x^{2k} - y^{2k} \text{ is divisible by } x + y.$

We will show that $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true, that is:
$\displaystyle x^{2(k + 1)} - y^{2(k + 1)} \text{ is divisible by } x + y.$

The proof is as follows:

$\displaystyle \begin{aligned} x^{2(k + 1)} - y^{2(k + 1)} & = x^{2k + 2} - y^{2k + 2} \\ & = x^2 \cdot x^{2k} - y^2 \cdot y^{2k} \\ & = x^2(x^{2k} - y^{2k} + y^{2k}) - y^2 \cdot y^{2k} \\ & = x^2(x^{2k} - y^{2k}) + x^2 y^{2k} - y^2 y^{2k} \\ & = x^2(x^{2k} - y^{2k}) + y^{2k}(x^2 - y^2) \\ & = x^2(x^{2k} - y^{2k}) + y^{2k}(x + y)(x - y) \end{aligned}$

Since $\displaystyle x^2(x^{2k} - y^{2k})$ is divisible by $\displaystyle x + y$ (from the assumption) and $\displaystyle y^{2k}(x + y)(x - y)$ contains the factor $\displaystyle x + y$, their sum is also divisible by $\displaystyle x + y$.

Therefore, $\displaystyle P(k + 1)$ is true.

(3) Hence, by principle of mathematical induction, the statement $\displaystyle P(n)$ is true for all natural numbers $\displaystyle n$.
